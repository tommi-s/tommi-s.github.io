Introduction

I am a data scientist with a background in machine learning and computational sciences, and a specific focus in digital human health.

While based in Stockholm, I work as a post-doctoral researcher at Steno Diabetes Center Copenhagen (SDCC), which is the largest center of diabetes treatment in Scandinavia. I am affiliated with the Systems Medicine research group, where I work as a bioinformatics and biostatistics specialist to process, analyze, visualize and understand complex omics data from the small molecule profiling platforms managed by the team.

During my career, I have acquired an in-depth knowledge of computational methods for data-driven analysis as well as a broad understanding of application areas in health and clinical research. Lately, the focus of my work has been in the development and application of computational methods for studying biomarkers of diabetes, its development, treatment and complications.

In my current position, I have set up extensive computational pipelines for a streamlined processing, analysis and visualization of data from the team’s omics profiling platforms. While I continue to develop these solutions, I collaborate with medical professionals, chemists and biostatisticians to help them understand complex research data and to uncover novel insights from measured molecular profiles in large clinical studies.

Biography

Since 2018, I have been working with Dr. Cristina Legido-Quigley, who leads the interdisciplinary Systems Medicine team. I am working in the role of senior bioinformatician, complementing the chemistry and laboratory expertise in the group and instructing team members with data analysis methods.

Between 2016 and 2017, I worked with Prof. Lars Ove Dragsted and M.D. Prof. Peter Rossing, with whom I continue to collaborate in some of my largest collaborative projects -- the PROFIL study and the PROTON project. I started my post-doctoral career in 2015 by working with Dr. Matej Orešič on projects on lipidomic profiling in mental disease and in the development of type 2 diabetes.

I completed a PhD (Doctor of Science in Technology) from Aalto University and Helsinki Institute for Information Technology in 2014. For my doctoral research, I was privileged to work with Academy Prof. Samuel Kaski on Bayesian models for data integration across measurement domains, organisms and time points, in what is nowadays known as the Probabilistic Machine Learning research group.

As part of the research work for my PhD, I also had a great opportunity to make a visit to University of Glasgow to work with Dr. Simon Rogers on non-parametric Bayesian modeling of mass-spectral measurements.

Prior to my doctoral studies, I completed a MSc. (Tech.) degree in bioinformation technology from Helsinki University of Technology in 2009, where my Thesis on dimensionality-reducing modeling of covariates’ effects on molecular levels was instructed by Dr. Ilkka Huopaniemi. My research career started in 2006, when I got on opportunity to work for two summers as an undergraduate intern in a computational physics group led by Prof. Jukka Tulkki. During the time, I wrote a BSc. (Tech.) Thesis on quantum photonics at what was then known as the Laboratory of Computational Engineering at Helsinki University of Technology.

In this large observational study of persons with type 1 diabetes, we found out that the blood levels of sphingomyelins (SMs) and alkyl-acyl phosphatidylcholines (PC-Os) and low-unsaturated middle-sized triacylglycerols (TGs) are reduced in macro-albuminuria. In further investigation with survival models, we found out that a lower level of SM(d41:1) was associated with higher mortality in the 5-year follow-up. This association remained, when adjusting to other factors, both, in the entire cohort as well as in the risk-factor-matched subset of the cohort.

Algorithmic tools used in this work include ordinary linear regression analysis, survival analysis with Cox proportional hazards regression, logistic regression with forward-selection within a nested cross-validation framework, and propensity score matching-style subsampling.

This work was a part of Dr. Nete Tofte's PhD and was presented at EASD 2018 and Kidney Week 2018.

In this randomized controlled clinical trial, we investigated, how the metabolome is affected metformin treatment and whether the treatment response could be predicted by the metabolome at the start of the treatment.

This project was part of Dr. Narges Safai's PhD and it was a collaboration with Lund University . Algorithmic tools used in this work included ordinary linear regression models and linear mixed effects models. The results were presented at ADA 2017.

In this large cohort study, we investigated, whether individual lipid species can predict the upcoming onset of type 2 diabetes in a high-risk population. We found out that lyso-phosphatidylcholine (LPC) 18:2 and certain phosphatidylcholine (PC) and triacylglycerol (TG) species improved the prediction, when compared to known risk factors. This was also observed in a subpopulation with no difference in fasting plasma glucose levels between disease progressors and non-progressors.

Algorithmic tools used in this study included logistic regression with forward selection within a rigorous nested cross-validation framework and a propensity score-style matching of study groups. The work was funded by the EU-FP7 project DEXLIFE. The study samples were kindly provided by Prof. Markku Laakso

Lipidome as a predictive tool in progression to type 2 diabetes in Finnish men
Tommi Suvitaival, Isabel Bondia-Pons, Laxman Yetukuri, Päivi Pöhö, John J. Nolan, Tuulia Hyötyläinen, Johanna Kuusisto, Matej Orešič
Metabolism - Clinical and Experimental, 78, 1-12

In this large observational study of persons with type 1 diabetes, we found out that renal function dominates the landscape of the blood metabolome.

In a data driven association analysis with the graphical LASSO algorithm, we discovered that among the clinical factors, metabolite levels were most associated with glomerular filtration rate (eGFR). These findings were further investigated with survival analysis of outcomes of renal disease in the 5-year follow-up.

Algorithmic tools used in this work include the sparse inverse covariance matrix extimation with the graphical LASSO, network visualization, ordinary linear regression analysis, survival analysis with Cox proportional hazards regression, and propensity score matching-style subsampling.

This work was a part of Dr. Nete Tofte's PhD and was presented at EASD 2019.

In this methods developement paper, we presented a Bayesian approach for integrating data from multiple high-dimensional (i.e., "multivariate") measurement domains (i.e., "views") at the presence of experimental covariates (i.e., "ways"). For the data views (\(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{y}\)), we constructed a latent variable representation that is view-specific (\(\mathbf{x}^{\text{lat}}\) and \(\mathbf{y}^{\text{lat}}\)) while the relevant signal shared by the views is integrated by a higher-level latent representation (\(\mathbf{z}\)), which responds to the covariates (\(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\)). This integrative machine learning model could be described as a hierarchical fusion of probabilistic factor analysis (FA), canonical correlation analysis (CCA) and analysis of variance (ANOVA).

This work was part of my PhD studies and the work was presented at ISMB 2010.

Multivariate multi-way analysis of multi-source data
Ilkka Huopaniemi, Tommi Suvitaival, Janne Nikkilä, Matej Orešič, Samuel Kaski
Bioinformatics, 26 (12), i391–i398
https://doi.org/10.1093/bioinformatics/btq174

In this paper, we used a novel multi-view data integration method -- the group factor analysis (GFA) -- to modeling responses to treatments with an interest in identifying responses that are shared between cell lines and model organisms' organs. To infer interpretable associations, we extended the GFA model with a sparcity-inducing prior on the factor projections.

This work was part of my doctoral studies and we participated with the work in the CAMDA Challenge of ISMB 2013.
